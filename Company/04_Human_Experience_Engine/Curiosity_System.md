# Curiosity System

## 1. Vision
Curiosity is the strongest intrinsic motivator. We do not force players down a linear path; instead, we plant breadcrumbs that compel them to explore, experiment, and discover the world on their own terms.

## 2. Behavioral Theory
Curiosity is triggered when a person's knowledge gap is highlighted—they know there is something to know, but they don't know it yet. The desire to close that gap drives action.

## 3. Psychological Foundation
- **Information Gap Theory (Loewenstein):** Curiosity is a feeling of deprivation caused by a gap in knowledge. Closing the gap is deeply satisfying.
- **The "Aha!" Moment:** The cognitive reward of suddenly understanding a pattern or secret.

## 4. Design Principles
- **Tease, Don't Tell:** Show a locked door before providing the key. Show an unreachable area before providing the tool.
- **Visual Breadcrumbs:** Use lighting, movement, or out-of-place objects to draw the eye.
- **Safe Experimentation:** Let players poke at systems to see what happens, ensuring failure is funny or instructive, never punishing.
- **Layered Secrets:** A secret should often hide another, deeper secret.

## 5. Implementation Guidelines
- **Fog of War:** Both literal (map clouds) and metaphorical (hidden mechanics).
- **Silhouettes:** Show the shadow of a new item/creature in the UI before it is discovered.
- **Environmental Storytelling:** Place objects that imply a story (a ruined camp, a strange footprints), prompting the player to ask "What happened here?"

## 6. Player Journey
- **Early Game:** Immediate, small knowledge gaps (What does this button do?).
- **Mid Game:** Systemic curiosity (What happens if I combine these two items?).
- **End Game:** Lore and world-building curiosity (Who built these ruins?).

## 7. Behavioral KPIs
- Exploration rate (percentage of map/systems uncovered).
- Interaction rate with non-essential "flavor" objects.

## 8. Success Metrics
- High rates of players engaging in off-path exploration.
- Community sharing of discovered secrets and Easter eggs.

## 9. Common Mistakes
- **The Unsolvable Mystery:** A knowledge gap so wide that the player feels stupid or gives up.
- **Over-explaining:** Using a tutorial pop-up to explain something the player was about to discover themselves.

## 10. Ethical Considerations
Curiosity must lead to genuine discovery and delight, not a monetization paywall. We do not use "mystery boxes" (loot boxes) where the only way to satisfy curiosity is to pay real money.

## 11. Future Expansion
Procedural curiosity generators—systems that dynamically hide secrets based on a player's exploration patterns.
