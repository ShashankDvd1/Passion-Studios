# Player Motivation

## 1. Vision
To build a world players love, we must understand *why* they play. This document categorizes the core drivers of human play and ensures our games cater to a diverse array of intrinsic motivations.

## 2. Behavioral Theory
Motivation is the engine of action. Without it, the best mechanics will be ignored. Motivation exists on a spectrum from purely external (doing it for a reward) to purely internal (doing it for the joy of the act itself).

## 3. Psychological Foundation
- **Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan):** Competence, Autonomy, Relatedness.
- **Quantic Foundry Gamer Motivation Profile:** Categorization of gamers into Action, Social, Mastery, Achievement, Immersion, and Creativity. (For Wonder Forge, we focus heavily on Immersion, Creativity, and Achievement).

## 4. Design Principles
### Core Motivations in Wonder Forge Games:
1. **Expression (Creativity):** The desire to customize, decorate, and leave a personal mark.
2. **Completion (Achievement):** The desire to fill bars, collect sets, and optimize layouts.
3. **Discovery (Immersion):** The desire to explore the unknown, find secrets, and understand lore.
4. **Nurture (Relatedness):** The desire to care for entities (plants, pets, NPCs) and see them thrive.

## 5. Implementation Guidelines
- Every major feature should explicitly target one of the four Core Motivations.
- A balanced game provides overlapping systems. E.g., Gardening appeals to *Completion* (growing all crops) and *Nurture* (caring for them daily), and feeds into *Expression* (cooking recipes to decorate a table).

## 6. Player Journey
Players often enter a game with one primary motivation but can be cultivated to enjoy others as they deepen their engagement (e.g., a Completionist learning to love Expression through housing).

## 7. Behavioral KPIs
- Segment distribution (percentage of players engaging primarily in Expression vs. Completion tasks).
- Feature adoption rates based on assumed motivation type.

## 8. Success Metrics
- A balanced player base where no single motivation type makes up more than 60% of the audience.

## 9. Common Mistakes
- **Monoculture Design:** Designing the entire game for "Min-Max Achievers" and ignoring players who just want to decorate and relax.
- **Motivation Clashing:** Forcing an "Expression" player to engage in stressful, timed combat to unlock a cosmetic chair.

## 10. Ethical Considerations
We must not use Extrinsic motivation (rewards, currencies) to force players to engage in activities they inherently dislike.

## 11. Future Expansion
Development of internal player archetypes specific to Tiny World, allowing for tailored CRM and LiveOps messaging.
