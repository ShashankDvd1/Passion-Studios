# Inspiration Atlas

## 1. Vision

This document maps the companies and products that have shaped Wonder Forge Studios' philosophy. For each reference, we define what we admire, what we take, and what we deliberately reject. This is not a competitive analysis — it is a strategic influences map.

We do not copy these companies. We study how they think, then apply those thinking patterns to our own unique context: AI-native, solo founder, behavioral design, reusable systems.

## 2. Problem Statement

Without explicit references, design discussions devolve into abstract debates. "The game should feel premium" means nothing without a shared understanding of what "premium" looks like. "We should be like Supercell" is incomplete without specifying which aspect of Supercell and which aspects we reject.

Unexamined inspiration leads to accidental imitation. Examined inspiration leads to intentional differentiation.

## 3. Goals

1. Document five studio inspirations and five product inspirations with specific takeaways.
2. For each inspiration, define what we take and what we reject — with reasoning.
3. Create a shared vocabulary for design discussions ("Nintendo-level polish" becomes a specific, measurable standard).
4. Identify the strategic gap we occupy — the space between our inspirations that no one else fills.

## 4. Non-Goals

- This is not a competitive feature analysis (future `24_Research`).
- This is not a market sizing exercise (future `20_Business`).
- We do not rank or score competitors.

## 5. Principles

1. **Learn from the best, but never worship them.** Every inspiration has flaws. Blind admiration leads to blind imitation.
2. **Take the thinking, not the output.** We study how Nintendo approaches polish, not which polish effects Nintendo uses.
3. **Our gap is our advantage.** The intersection of what no inspiration does well is where we build our identity.

## 6. Requirements

### 6.1 Studio Inspirations

---

#### Nintendo — The Craft Standard

**What we admire:** Relentless polish. Every interaction feels intentional. Game feel that makes simple actions satisfying. Decades of consistency in quality standards. Their games feel like gifts, not products.

**What we take:**
- The discipline to delay shipping until quality meets an internal standard that exceeds market expectations.
- "Surprise and delight" as a design philosophy — hidden details that reward attention.
- Accessibility without sacrificing depth — simple to start, rich to master.
- The belief that game feel (juice, feedback, responsiveness) is not optional polish — it is the product.

**What we reject:**
- Premium pricing model. We are free-to-play. Different economics.
- Hardware-first thinking. We are software-only.
- Secretive culture. We are transparent with our community.
- Slow release cadence. We ship continuously, not annually.

---

#### Supercell — The Small Team Model

**What we admire:** Small, autonomous teams ("cells") that operate with startup speed inside a large company. Kill culture — willingness to cancel projects that don't meet quality bar. Focused portfolio of long-lived games.

**What we take:**
- Small team philosophy as a permanent feature, not a temporary constraint.
- The courage to kill underperforming projects early instead of sinking costs.
- Long-term game operation — games are 10-year products, not 6-month content cycles.
- Player-first design ethos that coexists with strong monetization.

**What we reject:**
- Reliance on human talent at every position. We are AI-native.
- Genre focus on competitive/strategic. We are cozy/behavioral.
- High marketing spend per title. We target organic-first growth.
- Team-based development. We are solo founder + AI agents.

---

#### Moon Active (Coin Master) — Behavioral Mastery

**What we admire:** Deep understanding of behavioral psychology applied to game mechanics. Masterful use of variable reward schedules, social proof, and loss-aversion. Revenue generation from simple mechanics through behavioral design.

**What we take:**
- Systematic behavioral design as a core competency, not an afterthought.
- The understanding that simple mechanics + deep psychology = long-term engagement.
- Social mechanics that create viral loops through genuine social dynamics, not forced sharing.
- Data-driven iteration on behavioral hooks.

**What we reject:**
- Aggressive monetization that exploits loss aversion and gambling psychology.
- Pay-to-win mechanics that create unfair social dynamics.
- Notification pressure designed to manufacture anxiety.
- The core philosophy of engagement through compulsion rather than wonder.

---

#### Amazon (Leadership Principles) — Operational Rigor

**What we admire:** The Leadership Principles as a decision-making OS. "Working backwards from the customer." "Disagree and commit." Day 1 mentality. Bias for action. These are not platitudes at Amazon — they are hiring criteria, promotion criteria, and strategy filters.

**What we take:**
- Values as operational tools, not wall decorations. Our Core Values (`02-001`) are modeled on this approach.
- Working backwards from the player experience to determine technical requirements.
- Bias for action — decide with 70% of the information, not 90%.
- "Disagree and commit" — once a decision is made, full execution regardless of initial dissent.

**What we reject:**
- Scale-dependent strategies. Many Amazon principles work because of their market dominance. We adapt for indie scale.
- "Customer obsession" taken to mean "metrics obsession." We obsess over player feelings, not just behaviors.
- Warehouse-level efficiency applied to creative work. Game design requires space for exploration.

---

#### Google (Product Thinking) — Product Management Excellence

**What we admire:** Product management as a discipline. Structured approach to defining problems, measuring solutions, and iterating based on data. Design sprints. OKR framework. 10x thinking.

**What we take:**
- Problem-first thinking. Define the problem precisely before proposing solutions.
- OKR-style goal setting with measurable key results.
- Design sprint methodology for rapid prototyping and validation.
- Data-informed (not data-driven) product decisions that balance quantitative and qualitative signals.

**What we reject:**
- "10x thinking" in contexts where 2x improvement is the right target. Not every game feature needs moonshot ambition.
- A/B test everything culture that kills creative intuition. Some designs should be trusted, not tested.
- Corporate product management applied to creative products. Games are art and science, not purely science.

### 6.2 Product Inspirations (Emotional References for Tiny World)

| Game | What It Does Best | What We Take | What We Reject |
|------|------------------|-------------|----------------|
| **Animal Crossing** | Creates a living world that evolves with real time. Ownership feeling. Community. Gentle pacing. | Real-time world evolution. Personal ownership. Seasonal content. Low-pressure exploration. | Rigid real-time gates. Limited mobile accessibility. No meaningful monetization model. |
| **Coin Master** | Behavioral hooks. Social competition. Simple core loop with deep meta. Variable rewards. | Behavioral loop architecture. Social dynamics. Session-based satisfaction. | Aggressive monetization. Slot-machine core mechanic. Loss aversion exploitation. |
| **Stardew Valley** | Depth of content. Player agency. Emotional storytelling through systems. Craftsmanship. | Content depth that rewards long-term play. Systemic storytelling. Crafted-feeling world. | Premium model (one-time purchase). No live service. PC-first design. |
| **Monopoly GO!** | Mobile adaptation of beloved IP. Social mechanics. Event-driven engagement. Massive revenue. | Event-driven content cadence. Social engagement without requiring coordination. Accessible UX. | IP dependency. Aggressive IAP pressure. FOMO-driven event design. |
| **My Talking Tom** | Virtual pet emotional bond. Simple interactions with delightful feedback. Broad demographic appeal. | Emotional attachment to a living entity. Satisfying micro-interactions. Accessibility. | Shallow depth. Ad-heavy monetization. Limited progression. |

### 6.3 The Gap We Occupy

```
                     Behavioral Depth
                          ▲
                          │
            Coin Master   │   ◆ Wonder Forge
            Moon Active   │   Studios
                          │
     ──────────────────── ┼ ──────────────────── ►
     Exploitative         │               Ethical
     Monetization         │         Monetization
                          │
           Hyper-casual   │   Animal Crossing
                          │   Stardew Valley
                          │
                          ▼
                     Surface Engagement
```

**Our position:** Deep behavioral design + ethical monetization. This quadrant is nearly empty. Studios with behavioral expertise (Moon Active, Zynga) sit in the exploitative quadrant. Studios with ethical design (Nintendo, ConcernedApe) sit in the surface engagement quadrant. We combine the depth of the former with the ethics of the latter.

## 7. Acceptance Criteria

- [ ] Every inspiration has a specific "take" and "reject" list — no blanket admiration.
- [ ] The gap analysis identifies a defensible market position that no current studio occupies.
- [ ] A designer can reference this document to resolve "what would [inspiration] do?" questions.
- [ ] Product inspirations are emotional references, not gameplay templates.
- [ ] The document does not position any inspiration as wholly positive or wholly negative.

## 9. Risks

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| Over-indexing on one inspiration | Medium | Medium | Five studios + five products provides balance. No single reference dominates. |
| Inspiration gap is empty because it's not viable | Low | High | Validate with player research. Ethical + deep behavioral design is underserved, not impossible. |
| Inspirations become outdated | Medium | Low | Quarterly refresh. Research Agent monitors all listed companies for strategic shifts. |
